How to Import from China: A Step-by-Step Sourcing Guide

Home/Blogs/How to Import from China: A Step-by-Step Sourcing Guide

Importing goods from China is a powerful way for businesses to source high-quality products at competitive prices. However, navigating the complex world of international sourcing requires a structured approach to avoid pitfalls like supplier scams, poor quality control, and shipping delays.

In this comprehensive guide, we walk you through the essential steps to import from China successfully.

1. Identify Your Product and Target Market

Before contacting manufacturers, you must have a clear understanding of what you want to import. Create a detailed product specification sheet (or Tech Pack) including:

  • Materials and specifications
  • Dimensions and tolerances
  • Labeling and packaging requirements
  • Compliance and testing certifications required for your country

Having a precise spec sheet prevents communication errors and ensures suppliers provide accurate quotes.

2. Locate and Verify Reliable Suppliers

Finding a supplier is easy, but finding a trustworthy supplier is critical. You can search on platforms like Alibaba or Global Sources, but you must verify them:

  • Check Business Licenses: Ask for their business registration documents.
  • Assess Factory Capabilities: Do they manufacture the goods themselves, or are they a trading company?
  • Verify Certifications: Check if they have ISO, CE, or specific industry certifications.
  • Request Samples: Never place a bulk order without approving a pre-production sample first.

Using a professional, local sourcing agent can help bridge the gap, as they can conduct physical factory audits and ensure compliance on-ground.

3. Negotiate Pricing and Terms

When negotiating, focus not just on the unit price but on the overall contract terms:

  • MOQ (Minimum Order Quantity): Understand the manufacturer's volume requirements.
  • Payment Terms: Standard terms are 30% deposit and 70% upon completion (before shipping). Avoid paying 100% upfront.
  • Incoterms: Clarify whether the quote is EXW (Ex Works), FOB (Free on Board), or CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight). FOB is typically recommended for importers who want control over their shipping costs.

4. Implement Strict Quality Control

Quality issues are much cheaper to fix before the goods leave the factory. Set up:

  • During-Production Inspections (DUPRO): To catch errors early in the manufacturing run.
  • Pre-Shipment Inspections (PSI): An independent inspector should check a random sample of the finished goods using AQL (Acceptable Quality Limit) standards.
  • Container Loading Supervision (CLS): To verify that the correct quantity is loaded securely.

5. Arrange Logistics and Shipping

Decide on the most efficient shipping method based on volume and urgency:

  • Express Courier: For small samples and urgent document shipments.
  • Air Freight: For high-value, low-volume goods.
  • Ocean Freight (LCL/FCL): For bulk shipments. Choose between Less than Container Load (LCL) or Full Container Load (FCL).

Working with a reliable freight forwarder will ensure your shipping documents (Commercial Invoice, Packing List, Bill of Lading) are handled correctly.
